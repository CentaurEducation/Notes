-Observe: if you keep sbtracting each value from the previous one, sooner or later you get a constant value.

-This is the discrete equivalent to the fact that:
dnf(x)/dxn = 0 for some n if f is a polynomial
-This kind of data can be observed:
1) When an experimenter plots the kinetic energy of an electron moving near a fixed (still, not moving) long charged wire (with no current passing through it) as it moves towards the wire, against the distance it has traversed, under favourable circumstances (i.e., when the system is closed)
2) When an experimenter measures the frequncy with which he finds electrons at various distances from a ball of electric charge in a quantum mechanics experiment of sorts
-Both of these situations are related to the Legendre polynomials; as the name suggests, they are legendary
-They form- in various situations- the solution to Laplace's Equation, an all important equation in physics

"Discovering" Differential Equations

-What you just saw- the " dnf(x)/dxn = 0 for some n" statement is, of course, a differential equation
-You can see how easy it is to notice this- IF you lucked out, like Galileo, and got polynomial data (big if, as you can see)
-But, as it turns out, there are many situations where you can describe what you observe, with a differential equation
(-FYI, the discrete version of a differential equation is a difference equation)
-Most of the time, however, it will take you a LONG time to get a polynomial- and that will be the wrong answer too
-But the polynomial you get through this method is MUCH MORE useful than the Lagrange interpolation one
-That's because, if you are familiar with Taylor series, you will know, that a large class of functions satisfy a certain condition known as analyticity
-These functions can be written as ONE INFINITELY LONG POLYNOMIAL, which is actually the power series expansion, to wit the Taylor series
-How else do we know how to calculate sin x, cos x, ex etc.?
-The Taylor series actually seems to transcend calculus, because several of them were discovered before Newton's birth- one of them related to the story of Achilles and the Turtle
-Remember that thing young Turing did?
-The Indian mathematician Madhava did it centuries before the invention of calculus

import math
#Taylor series
def factorial(n):
    if n is 1 or n is 0:
        return 1
    else:
        return n*factorial(n-1)

i= 0
x= 1
e_x= 0
while 1 is 1:
    if x**i/factorial(i)>=0.000001:
        e_x= e_x+x**i/factorial(i)
        i= i+1
    else:
        break

print( "e^(",x,")=",e_x )

-The factor: e-E/kT is critical throughout physics.
-When American JW Gibbs rewrote the formalism for thermodynamics, and derived so much of it from the notion of the "grand canonical ensemble"- and the probability that a system of many particles is in a "microstate" with energy E at temperature T is given by e-E/kT
-Furthermore, the same factor, e-E(r)/kT is the density in space of a gas of particles that is almost "natural", being influenced by some potential E(r). This means that a coulombic potential will arrange a gas of charges that is almost natural, if you can make that, into a spherical density determined by these.
-These discoveries too were enunciated with differential equations: df/dt = kf for some constant k

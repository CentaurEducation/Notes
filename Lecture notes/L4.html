a)Can you "add" two points and get a third point?
I.E., Find a rule-of-thumb to solve the problem: If you have two points in a plane:
where is the point: (a+c,b+d)?
Answer: P1. Join origin and (a,b); Do same with (c,d)
P2. Draw line passing through (a,b) parallel to origin-(c,d) line
P3. Replace (a,b) with (c,d) and vice versa in step (2) and repeat
P4. The intersection point of these two lines is (a+c,b+d)


-This is the origin of vector addition

-Edmund Halley had an argument at the Royal Society
-Stormed out and went to friend Newton's house
-Told Newton that Royal Society is discussing rubbish: that the Sun was pulling planets towards it
-He thought that if the Sun pulled towards  it, the planets would get sucked into the Sun
-But Newton tells him that it is not rubbish
-The mistake Halley was making is in being too intuitive
-In Einstein and Infeld's "Evolution of Physics", they illustrate the mistake and say that the correction needs the language of vectors
-Halley imagined that the rule of thumb to determine how an object reacts to a force is
R1. Look at the direction of the force
R2. The new direction of the object's movement is in this direction

-But Newton proved that the correct way is:
R1. Look at the direction of the force, draw the "impulse" vector (the force, scaled by the time in which it acted on the object)
R2. Look at the direction of the motion of the object (the "momentum", though Newton himself called it "motion"), draw it's vector
R3. Add the two vectors
R4. The sum is the new motion (momentum) of the object

Multiplication of points
-From the time of Euler we have had a good rule of thumb by which:
1) You can multiply two 2D points and get a third 2D point
2) The multiplication is such that all the basic laws of multiplication of real numbers- commutativity( ab=ba), distributivity(a(b+c)=ab+ac) etc. are satisfied
3) You can even divide two points just like you divide two numbers
-The rule-of-thumb is as:
(a,b) X (c,d)= (ac-bd , ad+bc)
-Actually, don't need to memorise this- simple rule of thumb to multiply 2 points:
M1. Create a variable J, and rewrite (a,b) as a+bJ, (c,d) as c+dJ
M2. Multiply a+bJ and c+dJ as if they were two numbers
M3. Replace J2 by -1 wherever it occurs in the answer
M4. Simplify the expression
M5. The expression without J in it is the x-coordinate of the answer, and the rest (after removing J) is the y coordinate of the answer

-This is just complex multiplication, and some people write J some people write i
What does multiplying two points get us?
-If you multiply any point by a point on that unit circle we saw earlier- meaning a point of the form ( cos t, sin t )- then we get a point whose position vector is the first one's only rotated by an angle t.
-So, now we can stretch, compress, move (translate) and even rotate vectors by any amount
-Therefore to describe an object rotating in a circle, with an angular speed of w radians/sec, we can just write (cos wt,sin wt)X(a,b) for its position at any time t, if it started at the position (a,b)

What about multiplying points of higher dimensions?
-Hamilton was obsessed with this
-He finally had an epiphany while walking home from a party with his wife and found a rule-of-thumb to multiply 4 dimemsional points:
(a,b,c,d) X (e,f,g,h)
= (ae-bf-cg-dh, af+be+ch-dg, ag+ce+df-bh, ah+de+bg-cf)
-The invention of three units: i, j, k and a similar rule of thumb as above will free us from memorising this
M1. Write each point (p,q,r,s) as p+iq+jr+ks, for some imaginary algebraic variables i,j,k
M2. Multiply these like normal numbers M3. Replace the following expressions with the expressions on their right hand side: i2=-1
j2=-1
k2=-1
ij= k
ji= -k
jk= i
kj= -i
ki= j
ik= -j
M4. Simplify; then group together expressions with i,j,k and those without
M5. The result is your answer

-Indeed, since there is apattern to these replacement rules, this system of rules- quarternion multiplication is often used in computer graphics to rotate objects
